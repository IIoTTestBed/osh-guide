# Preliminary Remarks

Developing components freely and openly differs significantly in many of the challenges to be managed from the traditional, in-house development approach.
Many workflows, as they occur collaboratively over the internet in modern software development using version control systems, often have an internal (manual) corporate counterpart.
For instance, documents must undergo a release process, and subsequent adjustments must be integrated into the information base (merge).
Likewise, the topic of licensing plays a central role—however, from the opposite perspective. While copyright is decisive for the development of open-source components to allow other individuals to use, modify, and distribute them, patent law plays a crucial role in classical mechanical engineering, restricting these freedoms.
Yet, both worlds are united by the common goal of creating a defect-free product with the least possible effort.

This guideline is intended to help facilitate the easiest possible entry into the development of open-source components. Open source gives people, "by design," the opportunity to adapt things as desired at any time and at any point. At the beginning of a project and as a newcomer, however, this freedom can be highly obstructive.

!!! quote ""
    "But every project is different and requires its own structures."

Yes, that is correct. However, at the start of development, one usually does not yet know the "optimal" structure for one's own project.

Therefore, this guideline aims to create a **consistent starting point**. Building upon this with increasing experience, this structure can and, under certain circumstances, must be abandoned—for example, regarding a suitable branching structure or a versioning scheme.

Likewise, this guideline must be continuously adapted to the changing requirements of open-source hardware.
